What are the rivers and sea supporting layers?

For the first time, we have published additional layers rivers and sea flood risk alongside the Flood Zones. These show flood risk with defences and without defences, for present day and for climate change. We have published this new data to support developers in meeting the requirements set by national planning policy and guidance.

How have the river and sea layers been produced and why are there sometimes inconsistencies?

Our NaFRA2 system brings together local modelling and national modelling. In some locations all the layers are only from local models. In others they are only from the national model. However, in some locations the layers are selected from a mixture of both local and national models.

In most locations, relevant layers have been combined with other sources of data to create the flood zones. However, in some areas, our older flood zones have been retained while we work on improvements. In these locations the river and sea layers have been produced with our new data, whereas the flood zones continue to use our older data. These are the main reasons why inconsistencies sometimes exist.

What inconsistencies do the layers show?

In some locations, the new layers show inconsistent results. For example, these include:

  • 1 in 30 defended extents being bigger than 1 in 100 defended extents
  • 1 in 100 undefended present day extents being bigger than 1 in 100 undefended climate change extents
  • Defended extents being larger than undefended extents

These issues only exist in some locations. The Flood Zones are not affected by these issues.

We have produced these layers using a new process as part of our new national flood risk assessment (NaFRA2). NaFRA2 brings together local modelling and national modelling. In a given location:

  • all the layers could be from local modelling
  • all the layers could be from national modelling
  • some layers could be from local modelling and others from national modelling

The main reasons for inconsistencies in these layers are:

  • Results showing differences in modelling (e.g. between national and local modelling).
  • Results showing poor data quality. In some known locations

What are we doing about these inconsistencies?

We are analysing the new data to better understand these inconsistencies. Improvements and updates are already planned later in 2025 which will help to resolve many of these issues. We may make further updates to the Flood Map for Planning to more clearly highlight where we know inconsistencies exist.

How should the data be used?

Users of any flood risk data should always satisfy themselves that it is suitable for its intended use. No flood risk modelling data is perfect. To produce a suitable flood risk assessment, other datasets and additional assessment will always be needed.

We continue to have a high level of confidence in the flood zones. The flood zones are the statutory triggers for local planning authorities to check if they must consult the Environment Agency or apply standing advice. You can find out if the Environment Agency will be consulted on your application or if flood risk standing advice applies, by visiting risk assessments: applying for planning permission.


Datasets suitable for planning activities

Flood risk assessment

Inform a Flood risk assessment

You can use all the data layers to inform a Flood risk assessment.

Identify the need for Flood Risk Assessment

To identify the need for Flood Risk Assessment use:

  • flood zones 2 and 3
  • water storage areas
  • all rivers and sea, and surface water layers

Exception and sequential test

Inform application of exception and sequential test

To inform application of exception and sequential test use:

  • flood zones 2 and 3
  • defences
  • all rivers and sea, and surface water layers
  • water storage areas (if local planning authority’s strategic flood risk assessment defines as Flood Zone 3b)
Identify the need for exception test

To identify the need for exception test use:

  • flood zones 2 and 3
  • water storage areas (if local planning authority’s strategic flood risk assessment defines as Flood Zone 3b)
  • rivers and sea 3.3% (if local planning authority’s strategic flood risk assessment defines as Flood Zone 3b)
Identify the need for sequential test

To identify the need for sequential test use:

  • flood zones 2 an 3
  • water storage areas (if local planning authority’s strategic flood risk assessment defines as Flood Zone 3b)
  • all rivers and sea, and surface water layes

Local planning authority designation of 3b (functional floodplain) in strategic flood risk assessment

To Inform local planning authority designation of 3b (functional floodplain), in strategic flood risk assessment, subject to local agreement with the Environment Agency, use:

  • water storage area
  • rivers and sea 3.3%

Development incompatibility

To check development incompatibility – Table 2 PPG use:

  • flood zones 2 and 3
  • water storage areas (subject to local agreement with the Environment Agency)
  • rivers and sea 3.3 (subject to local agreement with the Environment Agency)

Trigger local planning authority to consult Environment Agency or apply standing advice

Data that can be used to trigger this process

Subject to the apply process set out in National flood Risk Standing Advice for local planning authorities:

  • flood zones
  • main rivers

If local planning authority’s strategic flood risk assessment defines as Flood Zone 3b:

  • water storage areas
  • rivers and sea 3.3%

All climate change rivers and sea layers if site location is FZ1, in which case, apply National flood risk standing advice for local planning authorities as if site were Flood Zone 2.
